# Usability

The usability principle states that end users should be able to use the AI tool to achieve clinical goals effectively, efficiently, and with acceptable cognitive load. A technically accurate AI system that clinicians cannot or will not use provides no clinical benefit.

## Usability recommendations

### Us1: Define intended use and user requirements from an early stage

Understand who the users are, what tasks they need to perform, in what context, and with what support. This informs all subsequent design decisions.

**→ See:** [Intended Use and User Requirements](../design/intended-use.md)

### Us2: Establish mechanisms for human-AI interactions and oversight

Design the interface between AI and user to support appropriate trust calibration, human oversight, and error correction.

**→ See:** [Human-AI Interaction Design](../development/human-ai-interaction.md)

### Us3: Provide training materials and activities

Prepare and deliver training that enables users to use the AI safely and effectively.

**→ See:** [Training and Onboarding](../deployment/training-onboarding.md)

### Us4: Evaluate user experience and acceptance with diverse users

Conduct usability testing with representative users covering different roles, experience levels, and demographic characteristics.

**→ See:** [Usability and User Experience](../evaluation/usability-ux.md)

### Us5: Evaluate clinical utility and safety

Demonstrate that the AI, used in clinical practice, leads to better patient outcomes.

**→ See:** [Clinical Utility and Safety](../evaluation/clinical-utility-safety.md)

## Human factors in healthcare AI

Healthcare AI operates in a high-stakes, time-pressured environment. Human factors research has documented how:

- Alert fatigue causes clinicians to ignore important AI warnings
- Automation bias causes over-reliance on AI even when it is wrong
- Poor interface design increases cognitive load and error rate
- Training gaps lead to systematic misuse

Usability is not a soft requirement — it is a safety requirement. Poor usability causes patient harm.
